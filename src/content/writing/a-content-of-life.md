# The Life I Live in My Teen Years

*Or: Why Your Past Self Wasn't as Dumb as You Think*

---

## The Thought That Keeps You Up at 2 AM

Picture this.

You're lying in bed. It's 2 AM. Your brain, instead of shutting down, decides to pull up a highlight reel of your most embarrassing moments from three years ago.

That one conversation where you said something *confidently wrong*.

That decision you made that now seems *obviously stupid*.

That time you thought you were the smartest person in the room — and now you realize you were probably the dumbest.

We've all been there. It's called **the hindsight bias**, and it's one of the most powerful — and most misleading — tricks your brain plays on you.

---

## The Problem with Judging Your Past Self

Here's the thing:

> **You are judging your past self with the knowledge and experience of your present self.**

That's not a fair fight.

When you made that decision two years ago, you didn't have the clarity you have today. You didn't have the lessons life has since taught you. You were operating with the information, maturity, and emotional state you had *at that time*.

And guess what? **That version of you was doing the best they could.**

---

## The "I Was So Dumb" Trap

Let's break this down with a simple example.

Imagine you're 16. You're at a family gathering. Someone asks you a question about politics, or career, or life in general. You answer with **absolute confidence** — because at 16, you know *everything*, right?

Everyone nods. Some smile. The conversation moves on.

Fast forward to today. You replay that moment in your head. And you cringe.

*"Why did I say that? I sounded so stupid. Everyone must have been laughing at me."*

But here's the truth:

**They probably weren't laughing at you.**

They were probably smiling because they remembered being 16 too. Because they've been there. Because they know — just like you now know — that being young means being confidently wrong about a lot of things.

---

## Why We Overthink Past Decisions

Overthinking past decisions isn't just about embarrassment. It's about **control**.

Your brain is trying to solve a problem that no longer exists. It's running simulations:

- *What if I had said something else?*
- *What if I had made a different choice?*
- *What if I hadn't been so stupid?*

But here's the uncomfortable truth: **You can't change the past.** And more importantly — **you don't need to.**

Because the person who made that decision is not the same person you are today.

And that's a *good* thing.

---

## A Simple Framework: The 3-Question Rule

Next time you find yourself cringing at a past decision, ask yourself these three questions:

1. **Did I have the information I needed back then?**  
   If the answer is no, then you did the best you could with what you had.

2. **Would I make the same decision today?**  
   If the answer is no, congratulations — you've grown. That's not a failure, that's progress.

3. **Does anyone else even remember this?**  
   If the answer is probably no, then you're the only one still thinking about it.

---

## What Your Past Self Actually Needs

Your past self doesn't need your judgment.

Your past self needs your **compassion**.

That version of you — the one who made the "dumb" decision, the one who said the "cringey" thing, the one who was *confidently wrong* — they were trying their best. They were navigating life without the map you have now.

And honestly? **They did a pretty good job.**

Because if they hadn't made those mistakes, you wouldn't be who you are today.

---

## The Takeaway

> *"You are the best version of yourself you've ever been. And the worst version of yourself you'll ever be."*

That's the paradox of growth.

You'll look back at today — at *this* version of you — and cringe one day too. You'll think, *"Why did I think that way? Why did I believe that? Why was I so sure about things I now know I was wrong about?"*

And that's okay.

That's not a sign that you're broken. That's a sign that you're **growing**.

So next time your brain decides to pull up that highlight reel of embarrassment at 2 AM, take a breath. Smile at your past self. And say:

*"Thanks for getting me here. I'll take it from now."*

---

### 💡 A Quick Exercise

Write down one decision from your past that you regret.  
Now write down **one thing you learned from it**.  
Now write down **one way that lesson has helped you since**.

You'll realize that the "dumb" decision wasn't really dumb at all.

It was just **tuition** for the person you are today.
