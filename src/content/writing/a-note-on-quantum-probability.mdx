This is a demonstration research note. It works through a standard, well-known textbook result as a personal exercise — it is not a claim of new scientific findings.

## Question

Given a quantum state written as a superposition of basis states, how do we get an actual probability out of it?

## Background

In quantum mechanics, a system's state is described by a wavefunction, often expressed as a sum:

psi = c1 * phi1 + c2 * phi2

where phi1 and phi2 are basis states and c1, c2 are complex coefficients.

## Method

The Born rule states that the probability of measuring the system in state phi_i is the squared magnitude of its coefficient:

P(phi_i) = |c_i|^2

For this to be a valid probability distribution, the coefficients must satisfy normalization:

|c1|^2 + |c2|^2 = 1

## Working Through an Example

Suppose c1 = 1/sqrt(2) and c2 = 1/sqrt(2). Then:

- P(phi1) = (1/sqrt(2))^2 = 0.5
- P(phi2) = (1/sqrt(2))^2 = 0.5

Which checks out — a fair 50/50 split, and the probabilities sum to 1 as required.

## Limitations

This note only covers the discrete, two-state case, and takes the Born rule as given rather than deriving or justifying it — that derivation involves measurement theory that's out of scope for a short note like this.

## What I Learned

Working the arithmetic by hand, rather than just reading the Born rule as a formula, made the normalization requirement feel like an actual constraint rather than a footnote.
